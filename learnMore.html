Additive Risk More Info
Nitrates & Nitrites Research suggests that regular consumption of nitrates and nitrites is associated with an increased risk of cancer. Used as preservatives in cured meats; e.g., salami, ham, bacon, sausages
Potassium bromate While Potassium Bromate is banned by many nations, it is still legal in the United States. The International Cancer Agency classifies Potassium Bromate as a 2B human carcinogen. It is known to cause tumors at multiple sites in animals, and also has been found to cause kidney and DNA damage. DISCLAIMER: Baking converts most potassium bromate to non-carcinogenic potassium bromide, but research in the United Kingdom has shown that bromate residues are still detectable in finished bread in small but significant amounts Used to strengthen bread and cracker dough to help it rise during baking; commonly added to flour
Propylparaben Research suggests that Propylparaben acts as a weak synthetic estrogen and may impact hormones for fertility. It also may alter the expression of genes, which could be associated with other health issues Used as a preservative in foods such as tortillas, muffins and food dyes.
Butylated hydroxyanisole (BHA) Research suggests that regular consumption of BHA could be linked to cancer. The National Toxicology Program classifies it as “reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen,” and the International Cancer Agency categorizes it as a possible human carcinogen. Added to fats and foods that contain fats (e.g., chips, preserved meats) and is allowed as a preservative in flavoring.
Additive Risk More Info
Butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT) While BHT is not a listed carcinogen, research suggests that it causes cancer, disrupts endocrine signaling, and causes developmental changes in animals. Used as a food preservative in things like cereals, sausage, hot dogs, meat patties, chewing gum, potato chips, beer, butter, and vegetable oils
Propyl gallate Despite being classified GRAS, there is some evidence that suggests propyl gallate is linked to an increased risk of cancer or endocrine disruption. The current evidence is weak, so Nutrinfo suggests you learn more before consuming this product. Used as a preservative in products that contain edible fats (e.g., sausage, lard), microwave popcorn, soup mixes, and frozen meals.
Theobromine Commonly found in chocolate and in leaves of the tea plant, and has effects similar to caffeine.
Secret Flavor ingredients “Natural flavors” & “Artificial flavors” Can contain synthetic material such as propylene glycol or the preservative BHA. Beware, flavor extracts derived from genetically engineered crops may be labeled as “natural.”
Additive Risk More Info
Artificial colors Different colors cause different health problems, for example, in a National Toxicology Program study, caramel colors III and IV have been linked to causing tumors. Commonly found in Instant flavored Oatmeal, salad dressings such as Kraft, cereals, syrup, and microwave popcorn. This is not a list of all foods containing Artificial Colors, just some of the most common.
Phosphate food additives “Phosphoric acid", “Sodium polyphosphate”, “Pyrophosphate", “Sodium tripolyphosphate”, “Polyphosphate”, “Tricalcium phosphate”, “Hexametaphosphate”, “Trisodium phosphate”, “Dicalcium phosphate”, “Sodium phosphate”, “Monocalcium phosphate”, “Tetrasodium phosphate”, “Aluminum phosphate”. Consumption has been linked to increased cardiovascular risk, and in people with chronic kidney disease, consumption is associated with heart disease and death. Phosphates are frequently added to highly processed foods, including fast foods, and are commonly found in breakfast bars, dairy creamers, flavored waters, and bottled coffee beverages.
Diacetyl According to OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration), Diacetyl has been associated with a severe and irreversible respiratory condition called bronchiolitis obliterans, which leads to inflammation and permanent scarring of the airways. Commonly found in butter flavoring in microwave popcorn, dairy products such as yogurt and cheese, and in “brown flavorings” such as butterscotch and maple.
Aluminum additives “Sodium aluminum phosphate”, “Sodium aluminum sulfate”. There is scientific uncertainty around the underlying effects of Aluminum additives, but it is thought to be linked to neurological effects such as changes in behavior, learning, and motor response. Aluminum additives are commonly found in cake mixes, processed cheese, baking soda/powder, and soy based and/or lactose intolerant baby formulas. This is not a list of all foods containing Aluminum additives, just some of the most common.

Red means research suggests that red labeled additives are associated with an increased risk of particular health risks.
Yellow means while these additives are classified as “Generally Recognized as Safe” or GRAS, they are not required to undergo pre-market review and approval. Research suggests that these additives are not safe for human consumption.
It is recommended that the user takes into regard of the known risk while consuming certain products.
